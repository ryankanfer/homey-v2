## Problem Statement

NYC real estate is designed to overwhelm buyers and renters. The entire process — from realizing you need to move to finally feeling settled — is the most stressful period in someone's life, and it's getting harder. Agents manage 10-20 active clients with spreadsheets and manual follow-up. Deals die from inaction. The lead gen market is commoditized: $75-150 per Zillow lead, same lead sent to 3+ agents, no profile depth.

homey. is a private real estate intelligence platform. Buyers profile themselves — timeline, budget, territory, psychology, fears. Agents get pre-profiled leads instead of cold contacts. The moat is depth, not speed.

## Demand Evidence

- Founder lived the buyer/renter chaos firsthand on both sides of the transaction
- Current workflow: spreadsheets + manual follow-up; deals die from inaction
- Multiple iterations of CRM tooling already attempted — persistent problem, not a passing frustration

Note: No external demand signal yet (no paying customers). Founder is the primary demand signal.

## Status Quo

Agents are cobbling together:
- Google Sheets to track client state
- Text threads for follow-up (no system, no prioritization)
- Memory for client preferences (fails as volume increases)
- Broker-provided tools if they have them (mostly unused)

Cost of the status quo: clients go cold, follow-up happens too late, deals that should close don't. The agent doesn't know which client to call today, so they call no one.

Buyers are navigating StreetEasy/Zillow with no synthesis layer — endless listings, no way to understand their own position, no one explaining the process.

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge

**Primary paying customer:** Independent agent with 10-20 active buyers/renters. No assistant. Everything runs through them. The deal dies when they get busy.

**Primary user (free):** NYC buyers who are underserved — not necessarily unrepresented. Includes buyers whose agent went dark, buyers early in search with no agent commitment, and buyers who found homey. independently. "Underserved" is a bigger and more accurate funnel than "unrepresented."

**Narrowest wedge:** The founder uses homey. for their own active clients (both agent CRM and buyer profiles). Every client interaction generates a data point. The product ships as an insight engine for the founder before it ships as a marketplace.

## Premises

1. **Agent pays, buyer uses free.** Buyer experience is the product. Agent access to buyer intelligence is the revenue.
2. **REVISED: Target is underserved buyers, not just unrepresented ones.** Buyers whose current agent is ghosting them, or who haven't committed to one yet. This is the correct and larger funnel.
3. **NYC is the right first market.** Highest transaction values, most complex process, most emotionally overwhelming, most agent-dependent at close.

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: CRM Weapon First (rejected — done before)
Build a Morning Brief / daily ranked client list for founder's own clients. Validate on self, then charge 5 agents $100/month. No cold-start problem. Effort: S. Risk: Low.
**Why rejected:** Founder has already built multiple CRM-first iterations. This is not a new path. It doesn't lead to the marketplace.

### Approach B: Buyer Acquisition First — Founder as Guinea Pig (APPROVED)
Launch buyer-facing product publicly. Founder uses it as a buyer themselves (and as an agent with their clients). Acquire 50-100 buyer profiles through organic/content channels. Then sell agent access to that inventory. Founder is simultaneously the product's first user and its first validator.
Effort: M-L. Risk: Medium (cold-start) but de-risked by founder's direct use.

### Approach C: Agents Bring Buyers In (considered, rejected for now)
Give 5-10 agents free CRM, have them send onboarding links to clients. Avoids cold-start. Risk: dependent on uncertain agent recruitment. May be the right Phase 2 move after initial buyer profiles exist.

## Recommended Approach

**Approach B — Buyer acquisition first, founder as the product's first guinea pig.**

The founder is an agent with real clients. They are not building a hypothetical product — they are building the tool they are actively missing. The buyer onboarding flow is built. The agent CRM is built. The next job is to use it in production with real transactions and accumulate buyer profiles.

The "founder as guinea pig" framing is the key clarification: this is not marketing-led buyer acquisition. It's production usage. The founder's own clients go through the onboarding. The founder's own agent practice validates whether the intelligence is useful. Only after that does the platform open to the broader NYC market.

This de-risks the cold-start problem by treating the first 20-30 profiles as validation, not as the business. Once validation is clear — once the founder's own deals run better because of it — the distribution story is real.

## Open Questions

1. **Buyer retention:** What does a buyer do on the dashboard *after* onboarding? What keeps them coming back before they're ready to transact? (Currently: anxiety map, vault, strategy — but are these compelling enough?)
2. **Agent matching trigger:** When does a buyer indicate they want agent matching? Is it a button, an inferred signal, or both?
3. **Competitive positioning vs. StreetEasy/Compass:** They have buyer tools too. What specific moment does homey. own that they don't?
4. **Ghost risk as a buyer signal:** Ghost risk is defined as: no meaningful agent-to-buyer contact in 7+ days when the buyer has an active search (timeline < 6 months). If homey. can detect this signal — e.g. buyer's `last_meaningful_touch` > 7 days + buyer has indicated an active timeline in onboarding — should homey. proactively surface them to other agents? What's the legal/ethical frame for surfacing a buyer whose agent isn't engaged?

## Success Criteria

- Founder uses homey. as their daily agent tool for 30 days straight
- 10 buyer profiles exist with full data (name, timeline, budget, territory, fear)
- Founder can articulate one specific deal outcome that went better because of homey. intelligence
- At least 1 person outside the founder's immediate circle has gone through onboarding without being asked twice

## Distribution Plan

- Agent acquisition Phase 2: once 50+ buyer profiles exist, approach 3-5 agents in founder's network with offer of pre-qualified lead access
